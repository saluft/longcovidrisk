Long COVID is keeping 4 million Americans out of work. Who's at risk?

Long COVID affects 1 in 5 Americans infected with the coronavirus. Research now points to who is most at risk for developing the condition.
By Sarah Luft
October 6, 2022
By Sarah Luft
October 6, 2022

Long COVID is estimated to be keeping 4 million Americans out of work, according to new data from the Brooking Institute. This number accounts for one-third of the current labor shortage in the US. Meanwhile, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates that nearly one in five Americans who've had COVID-19 are living with long COVID. This adds up to about 23 million Americans. The numbers are large. So, we looked into who is most at risk for developing the condition.

Long COVID is the umbrella term for new, lingering, and ongoing symptoms caused by a COVID infection. It's known to wreak havoc on the body. Symptoms often include cardiac, neurocognitive, sensory and nervous system dysfunction.

The condition can affect people of all ages following a COVID infection, even those with initially asymptomatic and mild cases. To date, studies show that middle-aged women are most likely to experience long-term symptoms. After following 200 patients for about three months, researchers uncovered four additional risk factors (regardless of gender) below.
